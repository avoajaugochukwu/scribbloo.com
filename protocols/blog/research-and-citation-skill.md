# Research & Citation — the discipline of primary sources

> "Studies show" is contraband. Every load-bearing claim cites a named source the reader can click through to verify.

The fastest way to fail Google's Helpful Content classifier — and to lose credibility with any reader who knows the topic — is to write paragraphs full of factual claims with no traceable origin. This skill is the discipline of doing it the other way.

For a coloring-and-drawing site, the cardinal sins are **a technique that doesn't work** (steps that produce a mess, or that skip the basic-shapes foundation), **a fabricated domain fact** (an art-history claim, a material property, or a color-theory definition with no authority behind it), and **implying an official license you don't have** (presenting fan-art of a trademarked character as endorsed by the rights holder). The internet is saturated with confidently wrong art content (made-up "color rules", invented art history, copied-around technique myths). Repeating them destroys credibility faster than any other error. Verifying every claim and confirming every technique before publishing is non-negotiable — see the verification gate below.

---

## Wrong techniques, unsourced facts, and bad trademark handling are the cardinal sins (the verification gate)

Before any post ships, every factual claim, every technique, and every licensed-character mention must be verified. This is a hard publish blocker.

For every load-bearing technique in a draft (a drawing step, a coloring tip, a printing instruction):

1. **Confirm it actually works.** Walk the steps in order; a beginner following them should get the result the post promises. The foundation-first rule (basic shapes → details, light guidelines before committing) is the standard for "how to draw" content — a tutorial that starts with fine detail is broken.
2. **Confirm the materials and terminology.** Correct vocabulary, used consistently, with units shown the way artists use them (see `materials-and-terminology-skill.md`).
3. **Don't invent certainty.** If an approach depends on the medium or the goal (cute vs realistic, markers vs watercolor), give both, not a false-universal "the right way."

For every domain claim (art history, material properties, color-theory definitions, named styles or movements):

1. **Find the authority** — a reputable art-instruction reference, a museum or art institution, an established technique guide, an art-supply manufacturer's spec sheet (for material facts), or a recognized art educator who states it.
2. **Confirm the fact and its conditions.** Many facts are condition-dependent (graphite grades behave differently on different paper; watercolor on hot-press vs cold-press); cite the condition, not a bare absolute.
3. **Classify the result:**
   - **Verified** — the technique works when followed, or the fact traces to an authority → publish with the claim and link.
   - **Range only** — sources or practice give a spread or multiple valid approaches → publish them explicitly ("cute, simplified style vs realistic, shaded style"), don't pick one as the only truth.
   - **Unsourced** — no authority and not demonstrable → cut it or flag it as a personal tip, never as confirmed fact.
   - **Fabricated / doesn't work** — a "fact" no authority supports, or a technique that fails when followed → publish blocker, full stop. Fix the technique or cut the claim.

Run this as the dedicated **accuracy & trust pass** (see `accuracy-and-trust-skill.md`). A draft with any unverified fact, broken technique, or mishandled trademark does not ship.

## Licensed characters & trademarks (a hard rule)

Some high-demand subjects are **licensed characters** (Hello Kitty, Pokémon, Sonic, Bluey, Spider-Man, and the like). The search demand is real, but the legal/trust line is not negotiable:

- **Never imply official or licensed status.** Don't say "official Hello Kitty coloring pages" or "authorized." Scribbloo is not affiliated with the rights holders.
- **Use descriptive, fan-style phrasing** — "fan-art style", "inspired by", "a cute kitty drawing" — and include a light disclaimer in the intro: *"Not affiliated with or endorsed by the rights holders — these are original fan-style drawings."*
- **Prefer generic equivalents** where the intent allows ("cute kitty" instead of a named character, "race car", "dinosaur"). When in doubt on a character page, flag it for review rather than publish.

This phrasing rule travels with every licensed subject through the whole pipeline — it is checked in the keyword brief (`keyword-research-skill.md`), the trust gate (`accuracy-and-trust-skill.md`), and the pre-publish audit (`google-trust-audit-skill.md`).

## What counts as a primary source

**Primary sources** are the original location where a fact — or a technique — was established. For a coloring-and-drawing site, the primary source of a domain claim is the museum, art-instruction reference, or manufacturer spec that established it; the primary source of a technique is whether the steps demonstrably work and align with established instruction. The hierarchy:

### Tier 1 — Always cite when available

- Museums and major art institutions (the Met, MoMA, Tate, the National Gallery) — for art history, movements, named artists and works
- Established art-instruction references and recognized drawing/painting authors — for technique, composition, and method
- Art-supply manufacturers' own spec sheets (e.g. a pencil maker's grade chart, a paper maker's weight in lb/GSM) — for material properties
- Color-theory references and recognized educators — for hue/value/saturation, complementary colors, and the color wheel
- University art-department and museum education pages (`.edu` / institutional) — primary teaching material

### Tier 2 — Acceptable for general-knowledge claims

- Recognized reference works and encyclopedias of art for tabulated facts (dates, mediums, movements)
- Major outlets reporting their own arts coverage (NYT, BBC arts desks) for non-technical context
- Official documentation from a standards or professional body (e.g. paper-size standards for A4 / US Letter print specs)
- Art-school or institution press material — but follow the link to the underlying course or exhibition page

### Tier 3 — Acceptable only when Tiers 1-2 are unavailable

- Wikipedia — **with care.** Use it only as a starting point, then follow its citations to the primary museum / reference / manufacturer source and cite that instead. Never cite Wikipedia as the load-bearing source for an art-history fact or a material spec.
- Reputable educational art sites run by recognized institutions or named, credentialed artists
- Encyclopedias (Britannica) for general background, not for load-bearing facts

### Tier 4 — Contraband

Never cite these as load-bearing sources:

- AI-generated content (ChatGPT summaries, etc.)
- Random "drawing hub" aggregators, content-farm "how to draw" listicles, and SEO spam — these are the single biggest source of copied-around technique myths and invented art "rules", and never count as proof of a fact or method
- "10 best art hacks" or "secret color rules" listicles on content-farm blogs
- Pinterest, Reddit, Quora, Medium posts (unless the author IS a recognized authority and the post is the original publication)
- Forum posts and unsourced "art tips" answers
- Any rights holder's brand site quoted to imply they endorse Scribbloo's fan-art — they don't
- Old archives of dead sites unless preserved by Wayback Machine
- Marketing copy pretending to be fact

## The attribution format

Every external claim takes one of two attribution shapes:

### Inline attribution (preferred for most claims)

> According to the [Tate's glossary](URL), Impressionism is characterized by visible brushstrokes and an emphasis on light over fine detail.

The link goes on the source identifier, not on the fact. The reader can verify by clicking the underlined text.

### Quoted attribution (for direct quotes from named sources)

> "Graphite pencils are graded on a scale from hard (H) to soft (B)," the [manufacturer's grade guide (Faber-Castell)](URL) explains.

Claim, source, role/context, publication, edition/date if relevant, link. Anything less is contraband.

### What an attribution must contain

| Element | Required? | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Source identifier | Yes | "the Tate's glossary of art terms" |
| Date / edition | Yes if the fact can change | "Met Museum timeline, 2023" |
| Publication / institution | Yes | "Tate" / "the Met" / the technique author's book |
| Author / body name | Yes if a named expert or author | "from Betty Edwards, *Drawing on the Right Side of the Brain*" |
| Role / context | Yes if helpful | "on cold-press watercolor paper" |
| Link to the source | Yes | `[anchor](URL)` |

## Fair-use quoting

Quoting from copyrighted source material is allowed under fair use, but the rules matter:

### Length limits

- **Up to ~90 words** for most cases — a short excerpt from a technique book or a museum page, used for commentary or analysis
- **Single short quotes** — a one- or two-line definition quoted with attribution is squarely fair use; cite the original publication
- **Longer excerpts** — quote sparingly and only as much as the commentary needs; for extended passages, quote a short excerpt and link out to the original
- **Reproducing artwork or a brand's character art** — don't republish a copyrighted image or a trademarked character's official art; describe the technique and link out, and for character subjects use original fan-style drawings with the disclaimer (see the trademark rule above)

### Always include
- Quotation marks (or blockquote formatting)
- Attribution (institution / author + original publication)
- Link to the source
- Edition / revision date where it pins down the fact

### When in doubt, paraphrase

If a passage is genuinely needed and you're unsure if you're within fair use, paraphrase and link out. Paraphrase still requires attribution.

## Outbound link discipline

Links are how trust is paid forward and inherited. Rules:

### Always link
- Every named source on first mention
- Every factual claim drawn from an authority (art history, material specs, color theory)
- Every expert / technique-book quote
- Every museum, movement, or named work referenced as fact
- Every product, art supply, or organization on first mention

### Anchor text rules
- ✅ **Descriptive:** "the [Tate glossary entry on Impressionism](URL)"
- ❌ **"Click here":** "...as found [here](URL)"
- ✅ **The named source:** "[the Met's timeline of art history](URL) notes..."
- ❌ **The URL itself:** "as discussed at https://..."

### Where to link
- Inline within the prose, not in a footnote stack at the bottom (modern web readers don't scan footnotes)
- The first time a source appears, with attribution
- On the relevant phrase, not on a generic word

### How to handle the link
- Same-tab by default (`target="_blank"` is a UX preference, not an SEO requirement; the project's layout decides)
- No `rel="nofollow"` on legitimate primary sources — that's a signal that you don't trust them, and you shouldn't be citing untrusted sources anyway
- `rel="nofollow"` only for sponsored links, paid placements, or untrusted user-generated content
- `rel="ugc"` for legitimate user-generated content links (forum posts, comments)
- `rel="sponsored"` for affiliate or paid links

### Internal links to coloring collections and tutorials

Link the matching coloring collection or sibling tutorial on first natural mention — e.g. `[rose coloring pages](/coloring-pages/flowers)`, `[how to draw a butterfly](/blog/how-to-draw-a-butterfly)`. These cross-links are both the reader's next step and the post's topical-authority signal; add the partner slug to `relatedCategories` / `relatedPages` in frontmatter.

## When sources contradict

Real research turns up disagreement. Don't paper over it. Handle it explicitly:

### Pattern 1 — Two reasonable sources disagree
> "Approaches differ: some tutorials build a rose from a central spiral, while others start from a teardrop bud and layer petals outward — both are valid; pick the one that matches the look you want." — give both and the condition, not one bare "correct" way.

### Pattern 2 — One source is clearly more authoritative
> "Hobby blogs sometimes call graphite 'pencil lead', but [a pencil maker's material guide](URL) confirms modern pencils contain no lead at all — graphite is a form of carbon — use that."

Cite both, but note which is current/authoritative.

### Pattern 3 — Sources contradict on a load-bearing fact
Don't pick a side without justification. A technique question is settled by what demonstrably works for the stated goal. For an art-history or material fact where authorities genuinely differ, write the disagreement *into* the post:

> "Watercolor 'lifts' (re-wets and removes) more easily on hot-press paper than on cold-press, but how much depends on the pigment's staining strength ([paper maker's guide](URL)); test on a scrap before committing."

This is a strong E-E-A-T signal — it shows you read enough to know the fact is contested or condition-dependent. Techniques, though, always defer to what actually works when followed.

## Quoting people who are not "experts"

Sometimes a useful quote comes from a non-expert: a learner, a parent, a teacher, a community member. These quotes are legitimate but follow different rules:

- Don't promote them to authority. "A parent who colors with her kids told us..." not "An art expert noted..." Never let an anecdote override a museum reference or a manufacturer spec.
- Get their consent before using their name. If they want anonymity, give them a descriptive identifier ("a kindergarten teacher who asked not to be named")
- Don't fabricate composite reader letters — Google's HCU treats this as scaled content abuse

If you're writing about something the *author personally experienced* (you actually drew the rose with a 2B pencil and it worked), that's a first-person experience marker, not a "quote." See `eeat-signals-skill.md`.

## The research log

Before drafting any post, the writer keeps a research log (in scratch space, not in the final output):

```
Topic: <target query>

Sources collected:
- [URL] — <institution/author> — <relevance>
- [URL] — <institution/author> — <relevance>
...

Domain claims collected (with source status):
- "<claim>" — <museum/reference/manufacturer> — [URL] — <verified | range only | unsourced>
- "<claim>" — <source> — [URL] — <status>
...

Techniques collected (confirmed they work):
- [drawing step / coloring tip / print setting] — walked the steps? produces the promised result? — <yes/no>
...

Trademark check:
- Is the subject a licensed character? <yes/no> — if yes, fan-style phrasing + disclaimer applied? <yes/no>

Open questions / contradictions:
- <fact/technique> — sources disagree: [URL1] vs [URL2]
```

The log feeds the post's outbound links and ensures every claim in the post — and every technique — traces back to something verifiable (a museum/reference/manufacturer for facts, a demonstrably working method for techniques).

## The WebSearch grounding pass (writer instruction)

Before writing the draft, the writer runs WebSearch on:

1. The exact target query (e.g., "how to draw a rose step by step", "what paper is best for markers")
2. The 2-3 most important domain facts/techniques in the brief (the key step sequence, the material spec, the color term)
3. "<material/term> art supply spec" or "<art fact> site:.edu" / "site:metmuseum.org" for each load-bearing fact
4. "<concept> definition" against a museum glossary or recognized reference to anchor each art term
5. The relevant convention where one exists ("hot-press vs cold-press watercolor paper", "HB vs 2B pencil")

Then collect ≥ 8 grounding bullets. Each bullet must include the URL. Use **WebFetch** to read the precise fact or definition from the authoritative page.

If WebSearch can only find:
- Random drawing-hub aggregators, "10 best art hacks" listicles, and content farms → the topic is too thin for a real post; tell the orchestrator
- AI-generated SERP entries on the first page → the topic is contaminated; pivot the angle or wait
- No museum / reputable reference / manufacturer for the load-bearing facts → mark the brief NEEDS MORE RESEARCH and stop

## The fact-check pass (writer instruction)

After drafting, for every load-bearing claim — an art-history fact, material property, color-theory definition, named style, or convention — verify it with **WebSearch + WebFetch** against an authority. Every domain claim also goes through the dedicated accuracy & trust pass (see `accuracy-and-trust-skill.md`); every technique is confirmed to actually work first, and every licensed-character mention is checked for proper fan-style phrasing.

Procedure:

1. **WebSearch** for the fact/definition at an authority, e.g. `Impressionism definition Tate`, `pencil grade chart Faber-Castell`, `US Letter vs A4 paper size`.
2. **WebFetch** the authoritative page and read the precise fact or wording.
3. **Patch the draft inline** if the authority contradicts or refines what you wrote. Patches are **literal swaps only** — never reorganize sections during this pass.

If the authority contradicts a claim that came directly from the brief / RESEARCH block, trust the RESEARCH (it is the curated brief) and note the disagreement at the end of the audit instead of patching.

Skip the fact-check pass entirely if the brief carries an explicit "research is final, do not fact-check" instruction.

## Citation density by type

How many primary-source citations should appear in a post:

| Content type | Minimum primary citations |
|---|---|
| 📝 Pillar / big listicle | 4-8 |
| ✏️ Tutorial (how-to-draw) | 2-5 (authority for each technique/material claim; the working steps for the method) |
| Concept / explainer (e.g. color theory, choosing supplies) | 4-6 (museum/reference/manufacturer for each definition or material fact) |
| Comparison / "vs" (markers vs colored pencils) | 4-6 (authority for each side of the distinction) |
| 📂 Coloring collection | 1-3 (print specs + any factual subject context) |
| 🛠️ Tool landing | 2-4 (file/print specs; any factual claims) |

A post below the minimum should be flagged in the audit. For concept and comparison posts, every domain claim that is not common knowledge needs a museum / reference / manufacturer source; every technique needs to actually work.

## Citation pitfalls the audit catches

- **Broken technique** (steps that don't produce the promised result, or that skip basic shapes) → publish blocker; rewrite the steps so they work
- **Fabricated domain fact** (an invented art-history claim, a wrong material property, a made-up "color rule") → publish blocker; cut it or source it
- **Implied license** (fan-art presented as official/endorsed for a trademarked character) → publish blocker; rewrite to fan-style phrasing + disclaimer
- **Fact sourced only to a drawing-hub blog or listicle** → trace to a museum/reference/manufacturer or downgrade to "a tip that works for me"
- **Wikipedia as the only source for a load-bearing claim** → fix by following the citation to the primary source
- **Claim without a link** → fix by linking
- **Dead links** → fix by replacing with archive.org or a fresh source
- **False certainty** (a single "right way" where approaches genuinely vary) → present both approaches
- **Wrong materials / terminology** (HB called "soft", lb/GSM dropped or mixed up) → rewrite per `materials-and-terminology-skill.md`
- **AI-summarized facts** (art history or material specs from an LLM with no source) → rewrite from a verifiable authority
