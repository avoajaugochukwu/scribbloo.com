# Materials, Tools & Terminology — Speak the Studio's Language

Scribbloo covers many corners of art — pencil sketching, colored-pencil work, marker and watercolor coloring, ink, and printable coloring sheets. Each medium and tool has its own native names and conventions, and a post reads as competent only when it speaks the studio's own language. A tutorial that calls a 6B "a number-six pencil", a coloring guide that says "thick paper" with no weight, or a post that swaps "hue" and "shade" as if they mean the same thing all signal an author who doesn't actually draw. This is a hard product rule, not a stylistic preference (see `blog-os-master.md` and `/CLAUDE.md`).

## The one rule

**Use the names, grades, and units native to the medium you're writing about, and carry them consistently from the materials list to the steps to the tips to the FAQ.** Never silently rename a tool mid-post. Never give a paper weight in only one unit system. When you introduce a term or grade, define it on first use, warmly and plainly — the reader is often a beginner or a parent.

❌ "Use a soft pencil." (which one? name the grade — "a soft 2B or 4B")
❌ "Print on thick paper." (how thick? give the weight — "cardstock, around 65 lb / 176 GSM")
✅ "Grab a 2B pencil (soft enough to shade, dark enough to see)."
✅ "Print on cardstock (about 65 lb / 176 GSM) so markers don't bleed through."

## The default ledger — names & units by medium

| Medium / area | Lead with | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Graphite pencils | the H–B grade scale: H (hard, light) → HB (middle) → B, 2B, 4B, 6B (soft, dark) | Name the actual grade, not "soft pencil"; define on first use. |
| Charcoal | vine/willow (light, smudgy), compressed (dark, dense), charcoal pencil | Note it's darker and dustier than graphite; smudges easily. |
| Ink / pens | fineliner with nib size in mm (0.1, 0.3, 0.5), brush pen, dip pen | Give the nib size; note waterproof vs. water-based for layering. |
| Markers | alcohol-based vs. water-based, broad vs. fine/brush tip | Alcohol markers blend and can bleed through thin paper — flag the paper weight. |
| Watercolor | pans vs. tubes, round vs. flat brush by number (e.g. #6), wet-on-wet vs. wet-on-dry | Behaves by water amount; lifts while wet, sets when dry. |
| Colored pencil | wax-based vs. oil-based, layering and burnishing | Layer light to dark; burnish to blend — name the technique. |
| Paper / cardstock | weight in **both lb and GSM**; sketch vs. marker vs. watercolor paper | Always show both units (see below). Heavier = less bleed-through. |
| Color theory | hue, value, saturation, tint, shade, tone, warm/cool | Define each on first use; don't use them as loose synonyms. |
| Page size / printing | **A4 (210 × 297 mm)** and **US Letter (8.5 × 11 in)**; "fit to page" | Coloring sheets print worldwide — name both sizes. |

The paper-weight rule: every time you state a paper or cardstock weight, **show both lb and GSM**, because a US parent thinks in lb and most of the world thinks in GSM — the same way a conversion is only complete when both units are named. Don't strip either unit from a weight, and don't mix scales within one figure.

## Grades, weights & precision — be specific, not vague

**Name the exact grade, size, or weight that matters, and keep it consistent.** The materials list is the reference: if you call for a 2B in the list, the steps say 2B, not "your pencil" or "a darker one".

Rules of thumb:

- **Name the pencil grade, not a vibe.** "Sketch with a 2B; deepen the shadows with a 6B." Don't write "use a soft pencil" when you mean a specific grade.
- **Paper weight always shows both units** — lb and GSM — and rounds to sensible whole numbers: "20 lb / 75 GSM" (standard printer paper), "32 lb / 120 GSM" (heavier, less show-through), "65 lb / 176 GSM" (light cardstock), "110 lb / 300 GSM" (heavy cardstock / watercolor). Don't invent decimals the spec doesn't have.
- **Don't fake precision.** "A4 is 210 × 297 mm" is exact; reporting "210.00 × 297.00 mm" is noise. A nib that's "0.3 mm" doesn't need "0.30000 mm".
- **Don't over-round either.** Calling cardstock "thick paper" loses the reader at the printer; keep the weight. Calling a #6 round brush "a medium brush" loses the size.
- **Carry the same name through the whole post.** If the materials list says "alcohol-based markers", the tips section says the same — don't drift to "felt-tips" halfway down.
- **State the convention when it matters** — paper weight depends on the paper *type* (a "90 lb" watercolor paper and a "90 lb" cover stock differ), so anchor lb to GSM, which is unambiguous.

## When to give both values vs. one

- **One value** when the figure is exact and universal: a pencil grade is just "2B"; an A4 sheet is "210 × 297 mm"; a brush is "a #6 round". These have one correct name — give it.
- **Both values** when readers split across systems or conventions: **paper weight** (always lb *and* GSM), **page size** (A4 *and* US Letter for a worldwide audience), nib size if you ever cite both mm and a brand's letter sizing. Name the dependency: "cardstock, about 65 lb / 176 GSM" or "print on A4 or US Letter".
- **Never** give a paper weight in only one unit, and never collapse two genuinely different sizes (A4 ≠ US Letter) into "standard paper". Universal facts get one name; split-audience facts get both.

## Consistent naming & defining on first use

- **Define every term, grade, and tool the first time it appears**: "a 6B pencil (soft and very dark)", "the hue (the color itself, like red or blue)", "burnishing (pressing hard to blend colored pencil)".
- **Use the canonical name** with its standard form: "2B", "HB", "#6 round", "0.3 mm fineliner", "65 lb / 176 GSM cardstock", "A4", "US Letter". The grade letter binds to its number: "2B", "6B", not "2 B".
- **Be consistent within a post**: don't write "colored pencils" in one paragraph and "pencil crayons" elsewhere without linking them once. Name on first use, then the short form is fine.
- **Use color terms precisely** — *hue* is the color itself; *value* is how light or dark it is; *saturation* is how vivid vs. dull; a *tint* is a hue + white; a *shade* is a hue + black; a *tone* is a hue + gray. Don't use them as loose synonyms.
- **Ranges use an en dash with the unit stated once**: "20–32 lb / 75–120 GSM", "2B–6B", "5–8 steps".

## How to present a materials list

A "What you'll need" list (e.g. in a tutorial) is high-stakes because the reader gathers supplies before they start. Every materials list follows this four-part pattern:

1. **Name each item with its grade/size.** Never leave a tool vague. "A 2B pencil", "an eraser", "printer or sketch paper", "colored pencils or markers (optional)."
2. **State the unit/spec where it matters, in plain text.** Paper gets a weight in both units: "20 lb / 75 GSM printer paper is fine; 65 lb / 176 GSM cardstock is even better." A brush gets a number: "a #6 round brush".
3. **Give a friendly substitution.** Beginners rarely own the perfect supply. "No 2B? Any everyday pencil works — it'll just be a little lighter." Show the swap, don't gatekeep.
4. **Note the printing setup for coloring sheets.** State both page sizes and the print option: "Print on A4 or US Letter, and choose 'fit to page' so nothing gets cropped. Cardstock (65 lb / 176 GSM) stops markers bleeding through." Where a spec is exact (A4 = 210 × 297 mm), say so and cite the source if needed (see `accuracy-and-trust-skill.md` and `research-and-citation-skill.md`).

A complete materials answer therefore reads: each item named with its grade/size, the weight in both units, a friendly substitution offered, and the print setup stated.

## Re-audit hook

Every post passes this materials & terminology check before it ships (folded into the master re-audit):

- [ ] Every tool/material is named with its grade or size (pencil grade, brush number, marker type), defined on first use.
- [ ] Names are medium-native (H–B grades for graphite, real medium names, correct color terms) and consistent across the list, steps, tips, and FAQ.
- [ ] No silent renaming of a tool and no vague "soft pencil"/"thick paper".
- [ ] Every paper/cardstock weight shows **both lb and GSM**; no missing unit and no false precision.
- [ ] Page size names both **A4 and US Letter** and states "fit to page" for printables.
- [ ] Color-theory terms (hue, value, saturation, tint, shade, tone) are used precisely, not as synonyms.
- [ ] Universal specs are given one name; split-audience figures (paper weight, page size) are given both, with the dependency named.
- [ ] Every materials claim and printing fact matches an authority (see `accuracy-and-trust-skill.md`).
