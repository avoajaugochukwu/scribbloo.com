# Conclusion & CTA — endings that drive the next action

> A blog conclusion is not a video outro. There is no "subscribe gesture," no end card, no algorithm watching whether the viewer clicks the next video. The conclusion has three jobs: re-anchor what the reader just learned, point to one specific next step, and leave them with something worth remembering — and, on Scribbloo, feeling like they *can* draw.

---

## The science (the parts that transfer)

### Peak-End Theory still applies

Readers judge an experience by its peak moments and how it ends. A strong conclusion retroactively makes the whole post feel better. A weak conclusion makes the whole post feel weaker, even if the steps were great.

### Action Window still applies

Readers are most likely to take an action in the 15-30 seconds *after* receiving value. Miss the window, lose the action. The moment a reader finishes their drawing and feels proud is exactly when they'll happily print the matching coloring pages — if you ask.

For blogs this maps to: after the wrap-up but before the reader closes the tab. That's where the single CTA sits.

### Decision Fatigue still applies

One CTA. Not three. Not "print these, share this, and follow us." Pick the action that *this specific post's reader* should take next, and ask for that one thing.

---

## What's different on the web

Three things the video playbook gets wrong for blogs:

### 1. No "thanks for watching"

Video viewers are passively absorbing a clip. Blog readers are actively scrolling — they don't need a thank-you. "Thanks for reading" at the end of a blog post is the gratitude trap; it provides psychological closure too early and tells the reader "we're done now," when actually you want them to draw the next thing or grab the printables.

### 2. Multiple CTAs in different forms

A blog post can have:

- **One in-line CTA in the body** — when the reader is at peak interest mid-post (e.g., "Happy with your cat? You can also [print the cat coloring pages](/coloring-pages/cat) and color this one in.")
- **One conclusion CTA** — the single primary action
- **A FAQ block** with internal links — different from a CTA; serves additional questions
- **Author bio with a link** — passive, not active

These are not competing CTAs. They are different things serving different reader states.

### 3. The FAQ block at the bottom

This is unique to blogs. It captures People Also Ask questions AND extends time on page. On this site the FAQ lives in the **body** as a `## Frequently asked questions` section with each question as an `###` heading — it is plain Markdown, hand-written in the post body, not declared in frontmatter. It's *not* a CTA, but it lives next to the conclusion and serves a related job: keeping the reader engaged after the steps have wrapped.

## The three-part conclusion shape

Every blog conclusion has three layers, in this order:

### Layer 1 — Synthesis (1-2 paragraphs)

Re-anchor what the reader learned. NOT a recap. A *re-anchor* — restate the central idea with the confidence the post just earned.

**Recap (bad):**
> In this post, we looked at how to draw a cat. We covered the head, the body, the ears, the face, and some variations...

**Re-anchor (good):**
> The whole cat comes down to two shapes and one habit: a circle for the head, an oval for the body, and the habit of keeping every line a little rounded. Place the eyes low and wide, and a stack of simple shapes turns into a cat you actually want to color.

The synthesis re-states what the post taught *as if the reader is realizing it for the first time*. They did the drawing; the synthesis pays them back with one sentence they can carry to the next animal.

### Layer 2 — Single CTA (1-2 sentences)

One specific action. Match the action to the content type and the reader's likely state.

For this site the default CTA is almost always one of two things: **"print and color the matching collection"** (turn the reader who just drew a cat into someone coloring a whole page of them), or **"try the related tutorial / explore the hub"** (send them to the next thing to draw, or the pillar). Match the action to the content type.

**By content type:**

| Content type | Best CTA |
|---|---|
| Tutorial ("how to draw a cat") | "Want to color one instead of drawing it? Grab the free [cat coloring pages](/coloring-pages/cat)." |
| Beginner explainer | "Ready to put it to work? Try the easy [how to draw a dog](/blog/how-to-draw-a-dog) tutorial next." |
| Comparison / "which way to draw it" | "Pick the cute version and warm up with the [how to draw a cat easy](/blog/how-to-draw-a-cat-easy) steps." |
| Pillar guide | "Start with the most popular subject in the hub: [how to draw a rose](/blog/how-to-draw-a-rose)." |
| Listicle ("40 easy things to draw") | "Found one you love? Learn the full steps in our [how to draw a butterfly](/blog/how-to-draw-a-butterfly) guide." |
| Coloring-led post | "Print a whole themed set from the [dinosaur coloring pages](/coloring-pages/dinosaur)." |
| Story-led pillar essay | "[Sign up](/email) to get the next drawing guide when it lands." |

### Layer 3 — One forward gesture (1-2 sentences)

The final note. Something worth remembering, and on Scribbloo, something encouraging. Often the strongest single sentence of the post. Often returns to imagery from the opening (Full Circle).

## Conclusion shapes (with examples)

The full patterns with examples:

### 9A — Full Circle

Returns to a specific image, fact, or scene from the opening. Reframes its meaning.

```
The "angry worm with wings" I opened with? That was just a dragon drawn all at
once. Break it into a bean body, a circle head, and two triangle wings, and the
worm becomes a dragon — one simple shape at a time. That's the whole secret,
and it works on every creature you'll ever want to draw.
```

Works when: the opening had a memorable specific image you can call back to.

### 9B — Unanswered Question

Closes the post's question but opens a friendly next one.

```
This post showed you how to draw a sitting cat from two shapes. The question
most people ask next is how to make it *do* something — pounce, sleep, curl up.
That's all about bending the same oval body into a new pose, and that's the next
guide.
```

Works when: there's a natural follow-up tutorial the reader can be pointed to.

### 9C — Quiet Landing

A single, simple statement. No hype. The plainness does the work.

```
A circle, an oval, two triangle ears, and eyes set low and wide. That's a cat.
Draw it once and you'll never be stuck for something to sketch again.
```

Works when: the post taught a method and a calm, plain coda lands harder than commentary.

### 9D — Wider Lens

Pulls out from the one drawing to the bigger idea.

```
This was never really about cats. It's about seeing the simple shapes hiding
inside anything you want to draw — and once you can do that, the whole world
turns into circles, ovals, and lines you already know how to make.
```

Works when: the post had a broader lesson you can land cleanly.

### 9E — Practical Synthesis

Tells the reader exactly what to do next.

```
If you want to lock this in today: draw the cat three times in a row. The first
will be careful, the second looser, the third actually fun — and by the third
you'll have the shapes memorized without trying. Then print a page and color
your favorite in.
```

Works when: the post is a tutorial AND has a clear "do this now" takeaway. Best for tutorials and how-to posts.

### 9F — Honest Acknowledgement

Acknowledges what the post doesn't cover.

```
This guide covers a cat sitting straight on, which is the easiest pose to learn.
It doesn't cover a cat in motion — running, leaping, stretching — where the body
oval has to bend and stretch. That's the next guide, and it's a fun one. [link]
```

Works when: there's a meaningful next step you didn't address that the reader will wonder about.

### 9G — Specific Recommendation

A single clear recommendation.

```
If you only remember one thing from this post: keep the eyes big and low on the
face. Everything else can be a little wonky and the cat will still read as cute,
but eyes that are small or set too high break the whole effect. Big, low eyes —
every time.
```

Works when: there's one tip that's clearly the most important takeaway.

### 9H — Restatement of Stakes

Restates why this mattered, with the confidence the post earned.

```
The reason "start with simple shapes" matters is that it's the difference
between a kid finishing a drawing proud and a kid crumpling it up. Build from
shapes and almost anyone, at almost any age, ends up with something they want to
keep. That's the whole point.
```

Works when: the post argued for *why* the approach matters, not just *how* to do it.

## The single CTA — phrasing rules

The CTA is one sentence. It points to one specific next action. Rules:

### Be specific
- ✅ "Grab the free [cat coloring pages](/coloring-pages/cat) and color this one in" or "Learn the steps in our [how to draw a dog](/blog/how-to-draw-a-dog) guide"
- ❌ "Check out our other posts"

### Match the reader's likely state
The reader has just finished a tutorial. They are:
- A little proud (they made a drawing)
- Either satisfied (it worked) or wanting more
- Open to *one more thing* but not *three more things*

The CTA matches:
- If they're happy with their drawing → "Here are the coloring pages to color it in"
- If they want more → "Here's the next thing to draw"
- If they're newly hooked → "Here's the hub where it all connects"

### Don't beg
- ❌ "Please subscribe / Don't forget to share / If you liked this..."
- ✅ "[Sign up](/email) to get the next drawing guide when it lands"

The CTA assumes the reader will act if they want to, not if you ask hard enough.

### Anchor text matters
The CTA link uses descriptive anchor text — usually the target keyword. Same internal-linking rules as the body. "Read more" is contraband; "Print the free dinosaur coloring pages" is right.

## The FAQ block (highly recommended)

Most posts benefit from a 2-4 question FAQ. This is NOT a CTA — it's the People Also Ask capture mechanism.

On this site, the FAQ is written directly in the **body** as plain Markdown: a single `## Frequently asked questions` heading, with each question as an `###` sub-heading and the answer as the paragraph beneath it. There are no FAQ components and nothing in frontmatter to declare. Place the FAQ block after the conclusion and before the author bio.

### Anatomy

```markdown
## Frequently asked questions

### What's the easiest way to draw a cat for beginners?

Start with two shapes: a circle for the head and an oval for the body. Add
triangle ears, a curved tail, and big eyes set low on the face. Keep every line
rounded and you'll have a cute cat in minutes. (40-60 words.)

### What pencil should I use to draw a cat?

An HB pencil is perfect for light sketch lines, and a softer 2B is great if you
want to add darker fur or shadows. One pencil is plenty to start. (40-60 words.)

### How do you make a drawn cat look cute and not stiff?

Round off every line and place the eyes big and low on the face. Small, high
eyes make a cat look serious; large, low eyes make it look sweet. (40-60 words.)
```

Each answer should be self-contained and lead with the direct answer, so it can stand alone as a People Also Ask result.

> **Schema note:** FAQPage JSON-LD is an *optional future enhancement*. The current renderer auto-emits only `BlogPosting` and does **not** auto-emit FAQPage structured data from the body FAQ, so don't claim the schema is generated. If FAQPage markup is added later, it should be wired up to read from these same body questions and answers.

### Question selection

Don't invent questions. Use real ones:

1. Search the post's target query in Google (e.g., "how to draw a cat easy")
2. Look at the "People Also Ask" box
3. Click each question — note which page Google currently surfaces
4. Write your own better, warmer answers for 2-4 of the most relevant PAA questions
5. Add those as `###` questions in the body FAQ block

Keep every answer correct and consistent with the steps in the post — same materials, same method, same proportions. Pull real questions from the keyword brief in `plan/` where one exists. This is the single highest-leverage move for catching long-tail traffic post-publish.

## Email capture timing

If the site has an email list, the email CTA can appear:

- **Inline at the end of the wrap-up** — soft, low-friction
- **As the conclusion's primary CTA** — higher commitment, for posts where the reader is genuinely hooked (usually story-led pillar guides)
- **In the FAQ block as a "subscribe to get future guides" line** — passive
- **In the author bio** — passive

Never all four on the same post. Pick the spot that fits the reader's state.

### Inline email opt-in

If an email opt-in is offered inline, write it as plain copy plus a markdown link to the signup route — there is no custom component to drop in. For example:

```markdown
One email a week — a new thing to draw or color, no filler.
[Sign up](/email).
```

Place it once per post, after the wrap-up but before the conclusion.

## The author bio at the footer

After the FAQ block, render the author bio. Quick version: a short 80-150 word bio for **Scribbloo** (the site/brand — `author` is `null`, which renders as "Scribbloo"), written as plain Markdown, with a single link to the about or coloring-pages page. Keep it warm and in-voice — Scribbloo is the friendly studio that makes free coloring pages and easy drawing guides for kids and grown-ups.

This is NOT a CTA. It is a trust signal. Keep it visually distinct from the conclusion.

## Common conclusion mistakes

### Mistake 1 — The summary recap
> "In this post, we covered the head, the body, and the face. Now you know how to..."

**Fix:** synthesize, don't recap. The reader was there. They don't need a list of what they just drew.

### Mistake 2 — Three CTAs at once
> "Print these, share this post, and check out our related guides..."

**Fix:** one CTA. Confused minds don't act.

### Mistake 3 — The gratitude trap
> "Thanks so much for reading this far! It really means a lot..."

**Fix:** never start the conclusion with thanks. It closes the reader prematurely.

### Mistake 4 — Empty platitudes
> "At the end of the day, art is just about practice and a little imagination..."

**Fix:** specifics or silence. Platitudes are AI defaults.

### Mistake 5 — No FAQ block
The post teaches the main drawing well but the reader's natural follow-ups (what pencil? how do I make it cute?) have no home on the page.

**Fix:** every post that *can* have an FAQ block, should — write it as a `## Frequently asked questions` section in the body. Tutorials, beginner explainers, and pillar guides especially.

### Mistake 6 — Premature email CTA
> "Before we begin, sign up for our newsletter to get more drawing guides!"

**Fix:** never CTA before delivering value. Conclusion CTAs work because the reader has already finished their drawing.

## Pre-publish conclusion checklist

- [ ] Synthesis section present (1-2 paragraphs, re-anchor not recap)
- [ ] Single primary CTA (one action only)
- [ ] CTA points to the matching coloring collection or a related tutorial/hub with descriptive anchor text
- [ ] Conclusion shape varied from the previous post's ending
- [ ] FAQ present in the body as `## Frequently asked questions` with `###` questions (2-4 for most posts)
- [ ] FAQ answers lead with the direct answer and are self-contained (40-60 words each)
- [ ] No FAQ in frontmatter; no claim that FAQPage schema is auto-emitted
- [ ] Author bio rendered after the FAQ block ("Scribbloo", from `author: null`)
- [ ] No "thanks for reading" gratitude trap
- [ ] No three competing CTAs
- [ ] Final paragraph reads like something worth remembering — warm and encouraging
- [ ] Every FAQ/conclusion detail matches the post's steps and materials (correct pencils, proportions, technique)
