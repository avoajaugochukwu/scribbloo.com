# Blog Intro Swipe File

## Opening paragraph patterns by intent

These are the patterns that win the first 8 seconds of attention on a Scribbloo post. The opening is the **top-of-body blockquote** (`> …`) and it has two jobs in order: **answer the query in 40-60 words** — lead with the easy first step, the promise, or the idea count — then **open a loop that earns the rest of the scroll**. Materials and terms are correct (HB vs 2B pencils, US Letter vs A4, "fit to page"), and every technique step actually works.

The patterns below are real working shapes for drawing tutorials, listicles, and coloring collections. Use them as models, not templates. Match Scribbloo's warm, encouraging "Storybook Retro" voice — talk to one reader, keep it simple, make them feel capable.

---

## CRITICAL: Conversational, not copy

The fastest way to spot AI writing is in the opening paragraph. Choppy fragments, "Most people don't realize", "Here's the thing", "Let that sink in" — those are AI tells.

Your opening should sound like a friendly art teacher talking to someone who's a little nervous about drawing. Not a copywriter writing ad copy. Not a textbook. A warm, specific human who has actually sat down with a pencil and knows it's easier than it looks.

The read-aloud test starts here. If the opening blockquote doesn't sound like something you'd say to a kid leaning over your shoulder, rewrite it.

---

## ANTI-AI SLOP: Opening contraband

| AI slop | Why it dies |
|---|---|
| "In today's fast-paced world..." | Generic. Earns no specificity. |
| "Have you ever wondered..." | Patronizing setup. Just ask the question. |
| "In this article, we will explore..." | Menu, not answer. Reader leaves. |
| "Most people don't know that..." | Condescending. Lazy. |
| "Whether you're a kid or a grown-up..." | Means nothing. Cut. |
| "Let's dive in." | Filler. |
| "Buckle up." | Filler. |
| "Here's what no one tells you..." | Pretentious. The reader can tell. |
| "What if I told you..." | Magician voice. |
| "Picture this:" | Cinematic preamble. Earn the scene. |
| "Art is the language of the soul..." | Sentimental warm-up. Lead with the first shape. |

## Pattern 1 — The Direct Answer Opener (tutorials, "how to draw X")

**Use for:** drawing tutorials and FAQ posts where the reader wants to start drawing right now.

**Shape:** Sentence 1 = the answer (the first shape or the simple method). Sentences 2-3 = the one tip that makes it look good. Reader gets the payoff AND a reason to scroll.

### Examples

**"How to draw a rose" (tutorial):**
> A rose starts as a small spiral, not a flower. Draw a loose swirl in the center, then wrap soft, curved petals around it — bigger and looser as you move out. The part beginners miss is that real petals overlap and never line up neatly, so the messier your curves, the more it looks like a rose.

**"How to draw a cat easy" (tutorial):**
> You can draw a cute cat from two shapes: a circle for the head and a soft oval for the body. Add triangle ears, a curvy tail, and two big dot eyes low on the face, and you've got it — no skill needed. The trick that makes it adorable instead of stiff is keeping every line a little rounded.

**Why this pattern works:**
- The reader can start drawing in 8 seconds
- It promises an easy win — exactly what a nervous beginner needs
- "The part beginners miss" / "the trick that makes it adorable" opens the next loop

## Pattern 2 — The Cold Open (story-led pillar guides, "what I learned" posts)

**Use for:** pillar guides, reflective "how I finally learned to draw X" posts, longform where the reader is here for the story as much as the steps.

**Shape:** Start mid-scene, in media res. Concrete, specific. The reader is dropped into a moment without context, and the post backfills as it goes.

### Examples

**Pillar guide opening (about learning to draw faces):**
> For years my drawings of people all had the same problem: the eyes sat too high, right up near the hairline, and every face looked startled. Then a kids' book illustrator showed me one rule — eyes go on the *middle* line of the head, not the top — and suddenly my faces stopped looking surprised. That was the week drawing people stopped feeling impossible.

**Reflective opening (about a drawing that finally clicked):**
> The dog I drew looked like a potato with legs, and my daughter had already named it. I'd put the eyes wrong, the snout wrong, the ears wrong. But the moment I started with simple shapes instead of trying to draw the "whole dog" at once, the potato turned into something she actually wanted to color. That shift — shapes first, details last — changed how I draw everything.

**Why this pattern works:**
- A specific, in-the-room moment hooks the reader who came for the story
- The skimmer sees the H2 below and decides whether to scroll back up
- "Changed how I draw everything" opens a stakes-bearing loop
- Trades quick-answer snippets for warmth and pull — only use when the story is the point

## Pattern 3 — The Choice-First Opener (comparison, "which way to draw it" posts)

**Use for:** posts where the reader is choosing between two approaches — cute vs realistic, pencil vs marker, simple vs detailed.

**Shape:** Sentence 1 = the choice that matters. Sentences 2-3 = who should pick A vs B in one breath.

### Examples

**Choice opening (cute vs realistic dog):**
> Whether you draw a cute cartoon dog or a realistic one changes everything about where you start. Go cute (big head, tiny body, huge eyes) if you want a quick, joyful drawing kids can color — go realistic (true proportions, fur texture, careful shading) if you've got more time and a softer pencil like a 2B. Pick cute first; almost every beginner gets a better result faster, and you can always level up later.

**Why this pattern works:**
- Names the real choice (two different drawings, two different feelings)
- Delivers the recommendation immediately, so nobody stalls
- "You can always level up later" is the reassurance that earns the rest of the post

## Pattern 4 — The Contrarian Opener (myth-busting, encouragement posts)

**Use for:** posts that exist to push back on a discouraging belief. Warm but firm, voice-forward.

**Shape:** Sentence 1 = the common belief, stated kindly. Sentence 2 = why it's wrong. Sentence 3 = what the post will show instead.

### Examples

**Contrarian opening (about "I can't draw"):**
> Almost everyone who says "I can't draw" believes drawing is a talent you're born with. After teaching beginners and watching kids go from stick figures to real animals in an afternoon, I've landed on the opposite view. Drawing is just a handful of simple shapes stacked in the right order — circles, ovals, and lines — and once you see the shapes hiding inside anything, the "talent" you thought you were missing turns out to be a skill you can learn in a single sitting.

**Why this pattern works:**
- Doesn't mock the discouraged reader — meets them with warmth
- "After teaching beginners and watching kids…" is a real experience marker
- "A skill you can learn in a single sitting" promises a specific, doable win
- The reader either pushes back (good — they read to test it) or feels hopeful (also good)

## Pattern 5 — The Story-First Opener (the story behind a subject or technique)

**Use for:** posts about the story behind a drawing subject, a technique, or a kid who learned something.

**Shape:** Sentence 1 = a specific moment. Sentence 2 = what was at stake. Sentence 3 = why it matters.

### Examples

**Story opening (a kid learning to draw a dragon):**
> When seven-year-old Maya first tried to draw a dragon, it came out as an angry worm with wings, and she nearly gave up. The day it clicked was the day we stopped drawing the "dragon" and started drawing a big bean shape for the body, a smaller circle for the head, and two triangle wings — building the beast one simple shape at a time. The scary, complicated dragon came apart into pieces a kid could actually draw.

**Why this pattern works:**
- Named person, specific moment, concrete scene (the bean body, the triangle wings)
- "Nearly gave up" is the hook — the gap between feeling stuck and finishing
- The contrast (the overwhelming whole vs. the easy shapes) is what the post explores
- The reader has to scroll to learn how the shapes come together

## Pattern 6 — The Specific Count Opener (listicles, idea round-ups)

**Use for:** listicles built around a number of ideas ("40 easy things to draw", "25 cute drawing ideas").

**Shape:** Sentence 1 = the count and what they'll get. Sentence 2 = what makes these ideas different. Sentence 3 = what the post will help them do.

### Examples

**Listicle opening (easy things to draw):**
> This list has 40 easy things to draw, and every single one starts from a circle, a square, or a few straight lines — no advanced skills anywhere. They're sorted into animals, food, nature, and doodles so you can jump straight to whatever your kid is into today. By the end you'll have a go-to drawing for any boredom emergency, and a few you'll want to print and color too.

**Why this pattern works:**
- The count is specific and the post must deliver it (promise the number, give the number)
- "Every one starts from a circle, a square, or a few straight lines" sets the easy bar
- The grouping promise (animals, food, nature) helps the skimmer find their spot
- "A go-to drawing for any boredom emergency" opens the next loop

## Pattern 7 — The Question Opener (FAQ, definition, beginner explainer)

**Use for:** FAQ posts and beginner explainers where the search query is itself a question.

**Shape:** Sentence 1 = restate the question more precisely than the searcher phrased it. Sentences 2-3 = the short answer with the helpful nuance.

### Examples

**Question opening (what pencil should a beginner use):**
> When people ask "what pencil should I draw with," they're usually asking two things at once. If you just want one pencil, an **HB** does everything well enough to learn on. If you want your shading to pop, add a softer **2B** for dark areas and keep the HB for light sketch lines — soft pencils (the "B" grades) go darker and smudgier, hard pencils (the "H" grades) stay light and crisp. One pencil to start; two to make it sing.

**Why this pattern works:**
- Refines the search query (signals you actually know art supplies)
- The two-track answer respects how beginners really shop
- The HB-vs-2B nuance is a useful, correct detail that earns the scroll

## Pattern 8 — The Practical Promise Opener (how-to, step-by-step)

**Use for:** tutorials where the reader wants a specific finished drawing.

**Shape:** Sentence 1 = the specific drawing they'll be able to make. Sentence 2 = how long it takes. Sentence 3 = a friendly credibility marker.

### Examples

**How-to opening (draw a butterfly):**
> By the end of this guide you'll have a symmetrical butterfly you'd be proud to color in, in about ten minutes, using nothing but a pencil and an eraser. The whole thing is built from one center line and four rounded wings — fold-and-match easy. I drew this one a dozen ways while figuring out the simplest path, so the steps here skip the fiddly parts and get you to a butterfly that actually looks balanced.

**Why this pattern works:**
- Specific outcome (not "you'll get better at butterflies")
- Time commitment quantified — ten minutes feels doable
- "I drew this one a dozen ways" is an experience marker + the real promise (the simplest path)
- The reader knows whether to keep going in 15 seconds

## Pattern 9 — The Friction Opener (tricky subjects, "this is hard" topics)

**Use for:** posts on subjects beginners find intimidating — hands, faces, horses — where they've tried before and felt frustrated.

**Shape:** Sentence 1 = acknowledge the reader's likely frustration. Sentence 2 = what this post will and won't do. Sentence 3 = the honest, encouraging claim.

### Examples

**Friction opening (how to draw hands):**
> If you searched "how to draw hands," you've probably already drawn a few that looked like bunches of bananas — hands are the thing almost everyone gives up on first. This guide won't pretend hands are easy, and it won't drown you in anatomy. What it will do is break a hand into a simple mitten shape first, then add fingers one at a time, with a step image for each — so you build a hand that finally looks like a hand, even on your first honest try.

**Why this pattern works:**
- Names the reader's frustration honestly and kindly (the banana hands)
- Sets expectations the post can keep — no overpromising
- "A hand that finally looks like a hand" is the friction-payoff promise
- High trust and warmth in about 60 words

## Pattern 10 — The Cross-Reference Opener (posts that link a tutorial to its coloring pages)

**Use for:** posts that sit inside a larger theme — a tutorial that pairs with a coloring collection, or a cluster post under a pillar.

**Shape:** Sentence 1 = the specific thing this post does. Sentence 2 = how it relates to the broader theme or the matching coloring pages. Sentence 3 = where to go next.

### Examples

**Cross-reference opening (draw a dinosaur, paired with the collection):**
> Drawing a dinosaur is mostly about one shape most people skip: a long, banana-shaped curve for the back and belly that gives the whole creature its stance. Get that curve right and a T-rex, a brontosaurus, and a stegosaurus all start the same way — only the head, legs, and spikes change. This post walks the steps; when you'd rather just color one in, grab the free [dinosaur coloring pages](/coloring-pages/dinosaur) and skip straight to the crayons.

**Why this pattern works:**
- Names the specific, useful idea (the back-and-belly curve)
- Drops the helpful nugget in sentence 2 (one curve, many dinosaurs)
- The internal link to the matching coloring collection establishes the tutorial↔collection pairing
- The reader gets the method AND a path to the printable

## The four opening mistakes the audit catches

### Mistake 1 — Burying the answer
> "Today, we'll explore the wonderful question of how to draw a cat and why drawing matters so much. To understand this, we first need to look at the history of feline art..."

**Fix:** lead with the first shape or the simple method. The reader can leave after one paragraph if you've satisfied them — that's a win, not a loss.

### Mistake 2 — Generic abstractions
> "The cat is one of the most beloved subjects in all of art. Throughout history, artists have been captivated by these graceful creatures..."

**Fix:** specifics. A specific shape, a specific step, a specific pencil, a specific tip. Abstractions are the AI default.

### Mistake 3 — Empty hype
> "What I'm about to share will completely transform your art forever. Most people have no idea about this one simple trick to never draw a bad cat again..."

**Fix:** the post either shows the easy steps or it doesn't. Stating it doesn't make it so. Lead with the substance.

### Mistake 4 — The lecture preamble
> "Before we begin, it's important to understand the fundamentals of line, form, and composition. There are many philosophies on what makes a drawing good..."

**Fix:** if a term needs explaining (like "value" or "HB"), define it inline as you use it. The preamble is throat-clearing.

## What every opening must contain

Regardless of pattern, every opening blockquote contains:

1. **The target query addressed** (often verbatim, often in a sharpened restatement)
2. **A specific element** (a shape to start with, a step, a tip, a correct material like "2B pencil" or "US Letter") that an AI couldn't have generic'd — and any technique detail is correct
3. **A signal of warmth and who's talking** (first-person, an experience marker, or the friendly "no skill needed" reassurance that is Scribbloo's voice)
4. **An open loop** (question, the "trick most people miss," or a promise that earns the next paragraph)

If any of those four is missing, the opening is weaker than it could be.

> **Trademark note:** if the subject is a licensed character (Hello Kitty, Pokémon, Bluey, etc.), the opening must use fan-style phrasing ("fan-art style", "inspired by") and never imply official status — add the light disclaimer that these are original fan-style drawings. Prefer a generic equivalent ("cute kitty", "race car") where the search intent allows.
